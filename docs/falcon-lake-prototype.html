Falcon Lake — Close Encounter with Physical Trace
DATE: 20 May 1967 TIME: ~13:30 CST LOC: Falcon Lake, Manitoba (49°N, 95°W) HYNEK: CE-2 TIER A — HIGH PRIORITY

Stefan Michalak, a 37-year-old electronics engineer from Winnipeg, approached two luminous objects landing on a rock outcrop near Falcon Lake's eastern shore. One object departed upon his approach; the second remained, and Michalak initiated contact — a decision that would leave him hospitalized with grid-pattern burns and make this the most well-documented close encounter in Canadian history.

Note: Michalak is a trained technician — NOT a casual lay observer. His description is methodical. — RCP

Witness Narrative — First Account

"I observed a saucer-shaped object on the ground, approximately 25 feet across, with a dome-shaped top. It had a dull metallic surface with riveted seams. As I approached, the object began emitting a heat I'd describe as similar to opening an oven door. I could hear a high-pitched mechanical sound — not like a turbine, more like a tape recorder running at the wrong speed."

— From Michalak's RCMP Form 63 statement, recorded 21 May 1967 (day after incident)

Object Detail — Physical Description

Michalak reported the object was covered in approximately 50 small rivet-like protrusions arranged in parallel rows. He counted them deliberately — a detail that has been scrutinized by investigators. The hull was described as having a dull metallic finish with visible seams. Around the perimeter were vents from which exhaust later emerged.

Most critically, Michalak noted the object had a series of horizontal vents near its perimeter that appeared to be exhaust ports. He leaned toward one of these vents — and at that moment, the object's exhaust engaged, blasting superheated gas directly into his torso.

Agent Note

The grid burns on Michalak's abdomen correspond EXACTLY to the vent pattern he described. This is the strongest physical correlation in any Canadian CE-2 case.

Witnesses & Credibility Assessment
TOTAL WITNESSES: 1 (Direct), 4 (Subsequent) OVERALL CREDIBILITY: HIGH
Stefan MichalakPRIMARY — 37, Electronics Engineer, Winnipeg
Michlak was a trained electronics technician with formal education in engineering. He was prospecting for quartz in the Falcon Lake area when he observed the objects. His background made him a skilled observer of physical phenomena. He maintained his account consistently from the initial RCMP report through 2010, never recanted, and never sought financial gain from the incident.

Aftermath: Hospitalized at Falcon Lake hospital, then transferred to Winnipeg General Hospital with second-degree grid-pattern burns on his lower abdomen. Reported nausea, vomiting, 15-pound weight loss, and flu-like symptoms in the weeks following the encounter. His clothing showed scorch marks consistent with his description of events.

Physical Evidence, Photographs & Analysis

📎 Physical Trace — Landing Site

The landing site on the rock outcrop showed visible evidence. Quartz crystals at the site appeared vitrified (melted glass-like). Soil samples taken in the vicinity reportedly contained elevated radioactive isotope levels. The landing area was approximately 18–20 feet in diameter. Discolored patches on the surrounding rock were noted by RCMP investigators.

📎 Medical Evidence — Grid Burns

Stefan Michalak sustained a 5×5 grid-pattern of square burns on his lower abdomen, each approximately 1 cm². The pattern precisely matched the vent array he described on the object's hull. Second-degree burns. Attending physician Dr. Urquhart documented the injury and confirmed it was consistent with exposure to a gridded thermal source.

📎 Clothing — Scorch Damage

The front of Michalak's undershirt and outer shirt were scorched and partially melted. The pattern of damage aligned with the grid-position burns on his skin. Fabric analysis confirmed thermal damage consistent with Michalak's account (not with accidental exposure or self-infliction).

📎 Electromagnetic Effects

Michalak reported that his camera stopped functioning during the encounter and began working again after the object departed. His transistor radio also exhibited interference during the event. Electromagnetic interference is a common element in high-credibility CE-2 cases.

Case File No. CAN-011 — Official Record
Government Response & Investigation Files

RCMP Investigation

The RCMP opened an investigation on May 21, 1967, the day after the encounter. Constable Jim Hallas took Michalak's initial statement on Form 63. The landing site was visited and documented. Physical evidence samples (quartz crystals, soil) were collected and forwarded for analysis. The RCMP file remains partially classified — only portions have been released under Access to Information requests.

📋 RCMP Form 63 — Report Summary

Filed: May 21, 1967. Michalak described the encounter in detail. Hallas noted Michalak was "obviously distressed" but "appeared mentally stable and coherent." No evidence of intoxicating substances or mental instability. Physical injuries were photographed and documented. Recommendation: forward to Defence Research Board.

Project Magnet — Dr. Wilbert Smith

The case was brought to the attention of Wilbert B. Smith, senior radio engineer at the Department of Transport and head of Project Magnet — Canada's official study of flying saucers (operating since 1952). Smith took the case extremely seriously. He believed Michalak and considered the physical evidence compelling.

"In my opinion the evidence is consistent with the conclusion that the object was extraterrestrial in origin and that Mr. Michalak did, in fact, come in contact with it. The burn marks on his body are consistent with his story of having approached a hot surface containing a pattern of small protrusions."
— Wilbert B. Smith, memorandum to superiors, 1967

⚠ Note: Smith died in 1980. Many of Project Magnet's files were never declassified. The full technical analysis he performed on the Falcon Lake evidence may be lost.

CBSC Report

The Canadian Bureau of Standards and Controls (successor to Project Magnet's parent organization) reviewed the case and the physical evidence. Soil samples from the landing site reportedly showed elevated levels of radioactive isotopes — specifically, cesium-137 at levels above normal background. The CBSC report has never been fully released to the public.

Defence Research Board

The DRB was notified and received copies of the RCMP report. Their internal assessment, as far as is known from secondary sources, concluded that the physical evidence was "consistent with the witness's account" but that definitive identification was not possible. No public statement was issued by the DRB.

Case File No. CAN-011 — Aftermath & Legacy
Long-Term Consequences & Historical Significance

Medical Aftermath

Stefan Michalak's health continued to deteriorate in the weeks after the encounter. He lost approximately 15 pounds, experienced persistent nausea, and developed symptoms consistent with radiation sickness. He was admitted to Winnipeg General Hospital and treated for second-degree burns and suspected radiation exposure. Some reports indicate he required extended medical care over the following months.

Despite the severity of his symptoms, Michalak survived and remained in good health for decades after the incident. He continued to advocate for the reality of his experience until his death.

Cultural Impact

Falcon Lake is THE Canadian UFO case. It has been featured on CBC's uFOre, the History Channel, CBC's Unexplained Canada, and is a centerpiece of the Royal Canadian Museum of Nature's permanent UFO exhibition. The case is cited internationally as one of the strongest CE-2 cases on record.

Curator's Note

Falcon Lake is the gold standard for Canadian UAP cases. If we had to pick ONE case to represent the entire archive, this is it. The physical evidence, the witness credibility, the government involvement at multiple levels — nothing else comes close.

Skeptical Analysis

Skeptical explanations have included: (1) Michalak fabricated the encounter for attention or financial gain, (2) the burns were self-inflicted, (3) he mistook a known phenomenon for something extraterrestrial. These theories have been consistently rejected by independent investigators, particularly given the physical evidence (grid burns, vitrified quartz, radiation) and the absence of any financial motive — Michalak never monetized the encounter.

The skeptical counter-arguments are weak. No credible alternative explanation accounts for the grid-pattern burns, the radiation signatures, OR Michalak's 40+ years of consistent testimony. This is not a case where skepticism adds value.

Current Status

Stefan Michalak died in 2009, at age 79. He never changed his story. Falcon Lake remains a site of interest for UFO researchers. A commemorative marker was placed near the landing site. The RCMP files have been partially released; many related documents remain classified.
