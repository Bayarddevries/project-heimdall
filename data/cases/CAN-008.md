# CAN-008: 1960 Clan Lake, NWT — The Crashing Sphere

**Date:** 1960
**Location:** Clan Lake, Northwest Territories, Canada (62.7°N, 114.2°W)
**Classification:** CE2 — Close Encounter with Physical Evidence

## Case Summary

In 1960, two prospectors working in the remote Clan Lake area of the Northwest Territories witnessed a spherical object descend rapidly and appear to crash into the lake. The event was accompanied by a loud crashing sound and produced visible disturbance on the water surface. The incident was reported to and documented by the RCMP.

## Historical Context — The Northwest Territories in 1960

The Northwest Territories in 1960 was frontier country:
- Mining and prospecting operations were expanding northward
- Communication was limited — no roads, limited radio contact
- RCMP detachments served as the primary law enforcement and reporting authority
- The region was sparsely populated, making witness testimony from the few people present highly valuable

Prospectors and miners working in the north were experienced outdoorsmen — the kind of people who were not prone to misidentify common objects.

## The Witnesses

The two witnesses were prospectors — experienced workers familiar with the northern environment:

- They would have been accustomed to aircraft, weather phenomena, and natural events
- Their proximity to the event was close enough to observe detail
- They were independent witnesses who corroborated each other's account

## The Event

The prospectors observed:

1. **A spherical object** in the sky above Clan Lake
2. **Rapid descent** toward the lake surface
3. **A loud crashing sound** as the object appeared to impact the water
4. **Visible disturbance** on the lake surface following the apparent impact

The event was dramatic and unambiguous — the witnesses did not describe vague lights or uncertain shapes. They reported a concrete event: a physical object descending and impacting the water.

## RCMP Investigation

The RCMP was notified of the incident and documented it. RCMP files from the era show that police took UFO reports seriously, particularly in the north where there were few sources of unusual events and prospectors were credible observers.

## Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Two independent, credible witnesses (prospector)
- Physical/environmental effects (water disturbance, audible crash)
- RCMP documentation
- Remote location eliminates common misidentifications
- Experienced outdoor observers

**Weaknesses:**
- No physical recovery of object from the lake
- Remote location limited investigation resources
- Lake depth/conditions prevented underwater search

## Agent Notes
This case is one of several "crash" cases in Canadian UFO history. Similar to Shag Harbour (1967) — where an object also entered the water — Clan Lake represents a pattern of objects descending into northern Canadian water bodies. The RCMP documentation lends credibility.
