# CAN-054: Lake Huron Object - Great Lakes Shootdown
**Date:** February 12, 2023
**Location:** Lake Huron, near the US-Canada border between Michigan and Ontario (45.2°N, 82.5°W)
**Classification:** CE1 — Military Engagement

## Case Summary
On February 12, 2023 — just 24 hours after the Yukon shootdown and 48 hours after the Deadhorse engagement — a fourth unidentified object was detected over the Great Lakes and shot down by a US Air National Guard F-16 Fighting Falcon. It fell into the ice-choked waters of Lake Huron and was never recovered.

This is the **fourth and final event** in the February 2023 North America Flap.

The Lake Huron case closed out an extraordinary eight-day period in which four unidentified objects were detected and destroyed over North American airspace — a concentration of military engagement with UFOs/UAPs that has no precedent in recorded history.

## Detection

The object was detected on February 12, 2023, by the same recalibrated NORAD radar and FAA civilian air traffic control systems that had flagged the previous three objects. Unlike the high-altitude stratospheric balloon (60,000+ ft) and the two Alaska/Yukon objects (~40,000 ft), this object was flying at the lowest altitude of all four incidents — approximately 20,000 feet.

The lower altitude made it easier to track on ground-based radar but also meant it was operating in airspace shared with civilian aviation, raising additional safety concerns.

## Engagement

A US Air National Guard F-16 Fighting Falcon from the **127th Wing, Selfridge Air National Guard Base** in Michigan was dispatched for the intercept.

Unlike the Yukon incident (which required three missile passes), the Lake Huron engagement was straightforward:
- The F-16 pilot successfully tracked the object
- One **AIM-9X Sidewinder** missile was fired
- The missile struck the target immediately, destroying it
- The object fell into Lake Huron

The lower altitude, combined with the fact that it was flying over a large body of water (no ground clutter from snow and terrain), made it significantly easier to track and engage than the Yukon object.

## Debris Recovery — Impossible

The crash site was on Lake Huron, near the international boundary between Michigan (USA) and Ontario (Canada). Recovery was impossible for two reasons:

1. **Ice coverage:** Lake Huron was approximately 90% ice-covered at the time, with thick, dangerous ice sheets
2. **Depth and temperature:** The lake's depth in the crash area was significant, and the water temperature was near freezing

The US Coast Guard and Canadian authorities conducted aerial and surface sweeps but officially **suspended active debris recovery** due to the hazardous conditions.

No physical debris was publicly confirmed recovered.

**Assessment:** Intelligence analysis relied on radar signatures, pilot descriptions, and flight trajectory data only. No forensic evaluation of physical materials was possible.

## Official Statements

**President Joe Biden:** Confirmed the F-16 shot down the object at his direction. He reiterated that these objects were benign and did not pose immediate kinetic threats to civilian populations.

**NORAD:** Stated the lower altitude allowed ground-based radar and civilian air traffic control to track the object more easily than the higher-altitude cases.

**White House:** Officials emphasized the objects were not connected to manned aircraft and posed no direct threat. No link between any of the four objects was established.

**Pentagon Brig. Gen. Patrick Ryder:** Characterized all three February 10-12 objects as likely benign — potentially commercial, recreational, or research balloons that had drifted into sovereign airspace.

### What This Means

The February 2023 flap revealed three critical facts:

1. **We were blind.** The radar recalibration that detected objects #2, #3, and #4 had never been in place before. This means similar objects may have been flying over North America for years or decades, undetected, because military radar was tuned to ignore them.

2. **Four objects in eight days.** The concentration of detections in such a short window is statistically remarkable. While the three smaller objects were assessed as "likely benign," the fact that they appeared immediately after the detection system was improved suggests they may have always been there — we just never looked.

3. **Fighter weapons aren't designed for this.** The Yukon engagement (3 missiles, 2 failures) demonstrated that modern air-to-air missiles are poorly suited to engaging small, slow, cold targets against ground clutter. This is not just a UFO problem — it's an airspace defense problem.
