# CAN-053: Yukon Territory Object - Cross-Border Shootdown
**Date:** February 11, 2023
**Location:** Yukon Territory, Canada — near Dawson City/Eagle Plains region (64.0°N, 137.5°W)
**Classification:** CE1 — Military Engagement

## Case Summary
On February 11, 2023 — the day after the Deadhorse, Alaska shootdown — a third unidentified object was detected crossing from Alaska into Canadian airspace over the Yukon Territory. It was engaged and shot down by a US Air Force F-22 Raptor in a remarkably difficult engagement that required three separate missile passes. The debris field landed on Canadian soil, triggering an unprecedented joint US-Canadian military recovery operation.

This is the **third event** in the February 2023 North America Flap.

The Yukon incident is notable for multiple reasons: it was the hardest object to shoot down (requiring three missiles), it landed on sovereign Canadian territory (requiring emergency cross-border military coordination), and it revealed just how difficult these small, slow-moving targets are to engage with fighter-borne air-to-air weapons.

## Detection

The object was detected by NORAD's recalibrated radar system as it tracked southeast from the Beaufort Sea across the US-Canadian border into the Yukon Territory, near Dawson City and Eagle Plains.

The radar recalibration (same system change that found the Deadhorse object) was specifically tuned to detect small, slow-moving objects that had previously been filtered out as ground clutter.

## Engagement — The Three-Missile Struggle

The engagement was one of the most difficult aerial intercepts in modern military aviation history. Two F-22 Raptors from the 3rd Wing at Joint Base Elmendorf-Richardson in Alaska were scrambled.

**Pass 1:** The lead F-22 pilot dive-bombed from 58,000 feet down to approximately 20,000 feet to obtain a visual and radar lock. The first **AIM-9X Sidewinder** missile was fired but **MISSED** — the target was too small and the missile's seeker could not distinguish it from the snowy, ice-covered terrain below (ground clutter interference).

**Pass 2:** The F-22 made a second firing pass. The second missile **FAILED TO DETONATE** properly or failed to track — a dud or seeker malfunction.

**Pass 3:** On the third pass, a third AIM-9X was fired. This missile successfully struck and destroyed the object.

**Total engagement time: approximately 45-60 minutes.** The entire sequence highlighted a fundamental weakness in current air-to-air missile technology — these weapons are designed to heat-seeking, high-speed jet aircraft, not small, slow-moving, cold targets against a snow-blanketed surface.

## Debris Recovery — Joint US-Canadian Operation

The debris scattered across a remote, heavily forested, snow-covered area near the frozen Porcupine River and Eagle Plains highway in the Yukon — some of the most inaccessible terrain in North America.

**Conditions:**
- Temperatures well below zero (-30°C to -40°C range)
- Deep snow cover
- Rugged, forested terrain
- Limited road access (Eagle Plains highway)

**Cross-Border Coordination:**
- The US and Canadian governments temporarily waived normal border/customs protocols
- US military personnel and recovery teams were permitted onto Canadian soil to work alongside the Canadian Armed Forces and RCMP
- Recovery efforts were slow and yielded fragmented pieces
- The debris was small-scale and scattered across a wide area

**Assessment:** No detailed forensic analysis was publicly released. US intelligence assessments relied primarily on radar data and pilot visual descriptions.

## Official Statements

**NORAD Commander Gen. Glenn VanHerck:** Confirmed the shootdown and stated the new detection systems worked as intended. The object posed no threat to civilian air traffic.

**White House (Press Secretary Karine Jean-Pierre):** Confirmed President Biden authorized the takedown. Emphasized the US and Canadian governments were working closely on recovery.

**Prime Minister Justin Trudeau:** Reiterated that the object was not a threat to public safety. Canadian officials coordinated with the US on the recovery effort.

**Pentagon Brig. Gen. Patrick Ryder:** Indicated the object was likely benign — similar to the Deadhorse assessment — with no evidence of hostile intent or espionage equipment.
