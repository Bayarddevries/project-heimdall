# CAN-052: Deadhorse Alaska Object - Beaufort Sea Shootdown
**Date:** February 10-11, 2023
**Location:** Deadhorse / Prudhoe Bay, Alaska — Beaufort Sea (70.2°N, 148.4°W)
**Classification:** CE1 — Military Engagement

## Case Summary
One week after the Chinese spy balloon was shot down over South Carolina, a second unidentified cylindrical object was detected over Alaska and deliberately shot down by an F-22 Raptor. This incident is the **second event** in the February 2023 North America Flap.

The object was detected by NORAD's newly recalibrated radar filters — the system had been adjusted after the Chinese balloon incident to detect smaller, slower objects that previously registered as "clutter" and were ignored. This recalibration is significant: it strongly suggests that many similar objects may have been flying over North American airspace for years undetected.

## Detection

Following the February 4 Chinese balloon incident, NORAD operators intentionally changed their radar filtering thresholds. Ground-based and over-the-horizon radars had previously been tuned to ignore small, slow-moving objects to focus on fast, high-priority threats (ICBMs, advanced aircraft). The new filtering was designed specifically to catch objects like the Chinese balloon — and it immediately began finding things.

NORAD Commander Gen. Glen VanHerck: *"We have updated our filtering... to make sure we don't miss anything that could be of concern."*

The object was detected on February 10, 2023, entering sovereign US airspace over the Beaufort Sea, north of Prudhoe Bay/Deadhorse.

| Color | Metallic |

## Engagement

**February 10, 2023** — US fighter aircraft scrambled from Joint Base Elmendorf-Richardson to intercept and visually confirm the object.

**February 11, ~2:30 PM EST** — On President Biden's order, an F-22 Raptor engages and destroys the object. The pilot fired a single **AIM-120C AMRAAM** medium-range air-to-air missile. The missile struck the object, causing it to break apart. Pilots reported no debris strikes to their aircraft.

## Debris Recovery — Failed

The remnants fell into the frozen, dark waters of the Beaufort Sea — one of the most hostile environments on Earth:
- 12-foot ice ridges
- Temperatures near -20°F (-29°C)
- High winds and polar darkness
- No deep-water salvage equipment in the region

The US Coast Guard, local tribal authorities, and military assets launched initial efforts but determined recovery was too dangerous and technically infeasible.

Secretary of Defense Lloyd Austin: *"The president directed us to bring it down in a way that preserves the debris, where possible, for analysis... We are going to continue to try to recover it."*

No significant physical components were publicly recovered. Intelligence assessments relied on radar signatures and pilot visual descriptions only.

## Official Assessment

By late February-March 2023, US intelligence concluded the object was most likely a **benign** — commercially or recreationally launched — high-altitude balloon (similar to those used by universities or hobbyists for atmospheric research) that drifted off course into sovereign airspace.

Pentagon Brig. Gen. Patrick Ryder: *"We do not rule out the possibility that it could be from a foreign entity... but we have no definitive indication that it was anything other than a benign research or weather balloon."*

No evidence of state sponsorship, espionage equipment, or propulsion was found.
