# CAN-034: 2021 Gulf of St. Lawrence — CADORS Dual-Aircraft Verification

**Date:** April 2021
**Location:** Gulf of St. Lawrence, Eastern Canada (48.0°N, 62.0°W)
**Classification:** NL — Aerial Anomaly | Dual-Crew Aircraft Verification

## Case Summary

In April 2021, a mysterious green luminous object was observed and reported through official Canadian aviation channels by crew members of two different aircraft operating in the Gulf of St. Lawrence region. The sighting was formally logged in CADORS (Canadian Aviation Occurrence Reporting System) under report number 2021Q2032, making it one of the best-documented Canadian UFO cases of the modern era due to its presence in an official government database.

This case gained significant media attention when journalists discovered the CADORS report and contacted the Department of National Defence for comment.

## CADORS

CADORS (Canadian Aviation Occurrence Reporting System) is Transport Canada's official database for aviation safety occurrences. Pilots and air traffic controllers are required to report any event that could affect flight safety, including unidentified aerial phenomena near their aircraft.

CADORS reports are considered highly credible for several reasons:
- Reporting pilots are trained aviation professionals
- Reports include time, location, altitude, and direction data
- The system is maintained by the Canadian government, not ufologists
- False or frivolous reporting is a serious offense

The Gulf of St. Lawrence incident was filed under CADORS 2021Q2032, meaning it was the 32nd reported occurrence in Q2 (April-June) 2021.

## The Event

According to the CADORS report and subsequent media coverage:

- A green luminous object was reported by the crew of one aircraft operating in the Gulf of St. Lawrence
- A second, independent aircraft crew also reported seeing the same object
- The object was described as a green light performing sustained, controlled flight maneuvers
- The object was visible enough to be noted and formally reported as a potential aviation hazard
- Time, date, and coordinates were logged in the official record

## Aircraft Verification

The significance of this case lies in the **dual-crew corroboration**. Two separate aircraft, presumably operating on different flight paths, both reported the same phenomenon. This eliminates several conventional explanations:

- **Not a reflection:** Reflections are aircraft-specific
- **Not a navigation light:** Aircraft navigation lights are well-known to pilots
- **Not a weather balloon:** Balloons don't perform controlled maneuvers at altitude
- **Not a mirage:** Mirages wouldn't be observed consistently by two separate crews

## Media Coverage

The case received media coverage when aviation researchers and journalists accessed the CADORS report:

- CBC News: "Military investigates green light over Gulf of St. Lawrence"
- Global News: Similar coverage citing DND response
- DND responded to media inquiries with a statement that they "monitor all NORAD-designated airspace" and "there was never any threat to Canadians"

## DND Response

When contacted by media, the Department of National Defence stated they were aware of the reports and had reviewed them. DND emphasized that there was no threat to Canadian sovereignty or civil aviation. However, they did not identify the object or provide an explanation.

## Assessment

**Strengths:**
- Dual-crew verification by trained aviation professionals
- Official government CADORS documentation
- Independent media corroboration
- DND acknowledged the report

**Weaknesses:**
- Full CADORS report text is partially redacted or limited in detail
- No photographic evidence published
- No radar confirmation data publicly released
- No follow-up identification ever announced
