# CAN-012: Shag Harbour UFO Incident
**Date:** October 4, 1967
**Location:** Shag Harbour, Nova Scotia (43.4°N, 65.7°W)
**Classification:** CE2 — Physical Evidence

## Case Summary
The Shag Harbour incident is the most famous Canadian UFO case and one of the best-documented unexplained aerial events in North American history. It is the only case officially classified as a "UFO Report" by the Canadian government. Multiple civilian and military agencies responded (RCMP, Coast Guard, RCAF, Royal Canadian Navy), and a three-day underwater search operation was launched. Nothing was ever recovered. Known as "Canada's Roswell."

## Timeline of Events

### Pre-Incident (Evening of October 4, 1967)

**~7:15 PM ADT — Air Canada Flight 305**
An Air Canada DC-8 (Halifax to Toronto) at 12,000 feet over Sherbrooke/Saint-Jean, Quebec. First Officer Robert Ralph and Captain Pierre Charbonneau observed a "brilliantly lit, rectangular object with a string of smaller lights trailing it" on a parallel course. At 7:19 PM, a sizeable silent explosion occurred near the object, followed two minutes later by a second explosion that faded into a blue cloud. Both pilots filed official reports. (Ledger 1998, p.72; National Archives RG 77 NRC UFO file N67-403)

**~8:00 PM — Mahone Bay Observation**
Darrel Dorey, his sister Kaykay, and their mother observed a large object maneuvering above the southwestern horizon from their front porch in Mahone Bay, ~100 km from Shag Harbour. Darrel wrote a letter to RCAF Greenwood Base Commander the next day. (Ledger 1998, p.75)

**~8:30-9:30 PM — MV Nickerson Radar Contact**
Captain Leo Howard Mersey aboard fishing vessel MV Nickerson at Sambro, NS (~28 km from the eventual crash site) observed four blips on his Decca radar that were stationary in the sky. Looking up, he and nearly 20 crewmen saw four bright objects in a roughly rectangular formation in the northeastern sky. Mersey radioed the rescue coordination centre and Halifax harbour master, and later filed a report with the Lunenburg RCMP. (Ledger 1998, p.73)

**~10:00 PM — Halifax Sightings**
Multiple people in Halifax reported glowing/flying objects around Halifax Harbour to the Chronicle-Herald and local radio stations.

**Official Telex:** RCC Halifax sent a priority telex to the RCAF Air Desk in Ottawa. The document was officially labelled a "UFO Report" — the only Canadian case to receive this classification.

### The Incident (~11:20 PM)

**~11:15-11:20 PM — Initial Sighting**
At least 11 people observed a low-flying lit object heading toward Shag Harbour waters. Multiple independent witnesses reported hearing:
- A whistling sound "like a bomb"
- A "whoosh"
- A loud bang

**~11:20 PM — The Descent**
Laurie Wickens and four friends, driving through Shag Harbour on Highway 3, saw a large object descending toward the waters off the harbour. From a better vantage point, they observed an object floating 250-300 metres offshore in Shag Harbour waters with lights still visible.

Wickens contacted the RCMP detachment at Barrington Passage to report what he believed was a crashed large airplane or small airliner.

### Immediate Response (11:20 PM - ~3:00 AM)

**~11:35 PM — RCMP Arrival**
Two RCMP officers arrived at the scene within ~15 minutes. The object, with lights still showing, began to sink before rescue could be attempted and disappeared from view.

**~11:50 PM — Local Fishing Boats Launch**
Cape Islander fishing vessels launched from Shag Harbour to search for crash survivors.

**~12:00-12:20 AM — Coast Guard Arrival**
Canadian Coast Guard search-and-rescue cutter arrived from Clark's Harbour (~1 hour after the crash). No survivors, bodies, or debris were found.

**~12:00-3:00 AM — Continued Observation**
Coast Guard Cutter #101 and local boats searched the area. RCMP Constable Ron Pound (who had independently witnessed the lights while driving on Highway 3, estimating a single craft ~60 feet long), Police Corporal Victor Werbieki, Constable Ron O'Brien, and other locals observed one yellow light bobbing slowly on the water, leaving a "yellowish foam" in its wake. The light eventually moved too distant to be seen or dipped into the water.

Search called off at 3:00 AM.

### Post-Incident (October 5-9, 1967)

**October 5 Morning — Aircraft Check**
RCC Halifax determined no aircraft were missing. Coast Guard cutter captain received radio message that all commercial, private, and military aircraft were accounted for along the entire eastern seaboard (Atlantic provinces and New England).

**October 5 — Official Filing**
The RCC Halifax telex to the RCAF Air Desk officially labelled the incident a "UFO Report." The RCAF Air Desk head sent a priority telex to Royal Canadian Navy headquarters recommending an underwater search. Navy dispatched Fleet Diving Unit Atlantic.

**October 6 — Press Coverage**
Halifax Chronicle-Herald ran front-page story: "Could Be Something Concrete in Shag Harbour UFO — RCAF" by Ray MacLeod. Squadron Leader Bain (head of Air Desk) was quoted: "We get hundreds of reports every week, but the Shag Harbour incident is one of the few where we may get something concrete on it."

**October 6-8 — Underwater Search**
Navy divers from Fleet Diving Unit Atlantic spent three days combing the seafloor. HMCS Granby was ordered to support the operation.

**October 9 — Search Called Off**
The Navy's final statement: "Not a trace... not a clue... not a bit of anything."

## Physical Evidence

- **Yellowish Foam:** A fishing boat captain reported seeing foam "yellowish in color" and "at least 80 feet wide" on the water surface. No samples were collected or analyzed.
- **Radar Contacts:** Captain Mersey's Decca radar registered 4 stationary blips matching the observed lights.
- **Official Telex:** The RCC-to-RCAF telex is the only Canadian government document officially classifying an incident as a "UFO Report."
- **Underwater Search Results:** Nothing found despite 3 days of diving operations.

## Investigation & Official Response

The incident triggered an unprecedented multi-agency response:
1. **RCMP** — On-scene investigation, witness interviews, official report filed (67-400-23-X)
2. **Canadian Coast Guard** — Search and rescue operation
3. **RCAF Air Desk** — Official government UFO classification, telex coordination
4. **Royal Canadian Navy** — 3-day underwater search operation (Fleet Diving Unit Atlantic, HMCS Granby)
5. **NORAD Baccaro Station** — Radar verification (no contacts matching the object)
6. **Rescue Coordination Centre Halifax** — Coordinated the initial response, verified all aircraft in the region were accounted for

## Skeptical Analysis

**Marine Distress Flares:** Brian Dunning (Skeptoid #565) argues flares could explain the sequential orange lights, whistling sound, and floating residue. However, the Coast Guard report stated they found "NIL RESULTS" for flares (no evidence found, not definitively ruled out).

**Meteor/Meteoric Bolide:** Meteors often break into multiple lights at oblique angles. Air Canada pilots' report of "silent explosions" with blue cloud is consistent with a bolide.

**Counter:** The underwater search found nothing, radar contacts were confirmed, and the RCMP/Navy response was genuine. If it were a simple meteor or flare, no such response would have been necessary.

## Later Claims (Styles/Ledger, 1993+)

These emerged decades later and are NOT part of the 1967 official record:

- Claim that the object traveled underwater ~25 miles to Government Point near HMCS Shelburne (a US Navy SOSUS support facility)
- Claim that the object was tracked on sonar by the Navy
- Claim a second UFO joined the first to "rescue" it
- Claim both crafts eventually surfaced and "shot away into the skies"

**Assessment:** These claims were made "off the record" by unnamed ex-military personnel. No official documents corroborate them. Styles and Ledger presented no documentary evidence in "Dark Object" (2001). Generally dismissed by skeptics and mainstream researchers as embellishment.
