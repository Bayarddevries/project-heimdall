# CAN-011: Montreal Fiery Serpents - February 8, 1662

**Date:** February 8, 1662
**Location:** Montreal, New France (present-day Quebec), Canada
**Classification:** NL — Nocturnal Lights | Historical Archive

## Case Summary
On February 8, 1662, the settlers of Montreal witnessed what the Jesuit Relations described as "fiery serpents" writhing and coiling through the sky over the fledgling French colonial settlement. This is the earliest documented UFO sighting in Canadian history — predating the modern UFO era by nearly three centuries, and representing one of the oldest aerial phenomenon accounts in North America.

## The Event

According to the Jesuit Relations of 1662 (Volume XLVII), on the evening of February 8th, colonists at the Montreal settlement observed multiple luminous objects in the sky "shaped like serpents," moving in what witnesses described as a writhing, undulating pattern. The objects appeared fiery — "burning" — and reportedly spanned significant portions of the visible sky.

The event was witnessed by multiple French colonists, missionaries, and Indigenous peoples present in the area. The Jesuit priests who recorded the account were trained observers and meticulous chroniclers — they took detailed notes not only on spiritual matters but on natural phenomena, weather, and unusual events as part of their mandate to document the New World.

## Historical Context

Montreal (then called Ville-Marie) in 1662 was a tiny French settlement of approximately 300-400 people, founded in 1642. Life in the colony was harsh, and the Jesuit Relations served as the primary means of reporting events back to Paris and the church hierarchy in France.

The "fiery serpents" were interpreted through a religious lens at the time — as celestial omens or signs, which was the standard interpretation for extraordinary sky phenomena in the 17th century. However, the level of detail in the Jesuit accounts — including the shapes, movements, and multiple witnesses — makes this one of the more compelling early UFO reports.

## Historical Significance

This case is important for several reasons:

1. **Earliest Canadian UFO sighting.** It predates any other recorded aerial phenomenon in Canadian history.
2. **Jesuit documentation.** The observers were trained, literate, and systematic chroniclers — not casual bystanders.
3. **Cross-cultural observation.** The event was witnessed by both French colonists and Indigenous peoples (Haudenosaunee/Wendat), though Indigenous oral accounts (if any existed separately) are not known to survive in the written record.
4. **Pattern recognition.** The description of multiple luminous objects in serpentine formation is remarkably similar to modern "string-of-lights" and "serpentine" UFO reports — the same shapes described in sightings over Shag Harbour (1967) and the Gulf of St. Lawrence (2021), over 350 years apart.

## Source Notes

The primary source is the Jesuit Relations of 1662, published annually in Paris. These documents are held in the BAnQ archives in Montreal and are available in both the original French and Latin. The account was also referenced in Father François Du Creux's *Historiae Canadensis* (1664), a comprehensive history of New France.

Modern ufological analysis of this case has been limited, partly because of the religious framing of the original accounts and partly because of the difficulty of extracting objective observational data from 17th-century theological language.
