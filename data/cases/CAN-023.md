# CAN-023: Charlie Redstar - Carman UFO Phenomenon
**Date:** October 26, 1974 - March 1975 (active ~5 months, peak Nov 1974 - Jan 1975)
**Location:** Carman, Manitoba (population ~2,500) and surrounding region (49.5°N, 98.0°W)
**Classification:** NL — Nocturnal Lights / Recurring Aerial Phenomenon

## Case Summary
The "Charlie Redstar" phenomenon was one of Canada's most famous and widely reported UFO incidents. From late October 1974 through mid-March 1975, deep crimson red lights appeared repeatedly in the night sky over Carman, Manitoba, and the surrounding prairie region. The phenomenon generated over 600 documented sightings (with popular reports claiming "thousands"), transformed a small prairie town into a national tourist destination, and became one of the most publicized UFO events in Canadian history.

The name "Charlie Redstar" was coined by the local newspaper — the *Carman Valley Leader* — after original witness **Len Grant**, a local farmer, first reported seeing the red lights on approximately October 26, 1974.

## The Original Witness

**Len Grant**, a farmer from the Carman area, first observed two red lights rising into the sky approximately 10 miles north of Carman on the evening of October 26, 1974. When the phenomenon gained explosive media attention, Grant became the face of the incident.

By early January 1975, he was so overwhelmed by phone calls (including international press) and visitors flooding his property that he stopped answering his phone and eventually changed his number to escape harassment. He essentially withdrew from public life as the phenomenon reached its peak.

## Description of the Phenomenon

The lights were described with remarkable consistency across hundreds of independent reports:

### Visual Characteristics

| Feature | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| Color | Deep, intense crimson red — no other color was ever reported |
| Shape | Spherical, dome-shaped, or disk-like "red orb" |
| Size | Basketball-sized to 3-6 feet diameter (some reports described larger) |
| Altitude | Approximately 5,000 feet |
| Luminosity | Intense, piercing red light visible for miles |

### Movement Patterns

- Initially moved in a **figure-8 pattern**, then would hover or drift slowly
- Made sudden, impossibly fast **acceleration and sharp direction changes** that defied conventional physics
- Would appear suddenly, hover, and then dart away at tremendous speed — shrinking to a tiny dot in seconds
- Described as moving **"against the wind"**
- Often appeared directly overhead and seemed to **follow observers**
- Sometimes described as **"dancing"** across the sky in formation
- Would **pulse, strobe, or throb**
- Often observed in groups or accompanied by multiple secondary objects

### Other Characteristics

- No sound or only a faint low hum / buzzing
- No visible means of propulsion, wings, or exhaust
- Left no physical trace, radiation, or landing marks
- Consistently observed over weeks and months by diverse, independent witnesses

## Scale and Duration

**Timeline:**
- **First Sighting:** ~October 26, 1974 (Len Grant)
- **Peak Activity:** Late November 1974 through mid-January 1975 (almost nightly occurrences)
- **Decline:** February-March 1975
- **Final Reports:** Sporadic sightings into April 1975
- **Total Duration:** Approximately 5-6 months of active phenomenon

**Sighting Volume:**
- **600+ documented sightings** officially recorded by RCMP, Carman UFO Society, and Redstar hotline
- Popular media claimed "thousands of sightings" from "tens of thousands of people"
- **24-hour hotline** established at the Carman Legion Hall, receiving hundreds of calls daily
- Callers were instructed by phone when a sighting was about to occur
- Switchboard flooded with reports from Manitoba, Saskatchewan, Ontario, and beyond
- Estimated **10,000+ newspaper headlines** generated in Canada alone

## The Hotline

The Carman Legion Hall became the operational center of the phenomenon:

- A dedicated **24-hour Redstar hotline** was established
- Trained spotters monitored the sky and called the hotline when lights appeared
- Callers would be alerted to look for the phenomenon
- The system created a network of hundreds of observers across the region
- Some researchers argue this created a feedback loop — people told to expect something were more likely to interpret ordinary phenomena (cars, planes, stars) as extraordinary

## Community Impact

Carman was transformed overnight from a quiet prairie town to a national tourist destination:

### Tourism and Economic Impact
- Hotels, motels, and campgrounds were completely booked solid for months
- Tour buses and private vehicles flooded into the small town (pop. ~2,500)
- Local businesses were overwhelmed with "UFO tourists"
- A "Charlie Redstar Festival" was organized to capitalize on the attention
- Estimated thousands of visitors from across Canada, the United States, and internationally

### Social Disruption
- Len Grant changed his phone number to avoid press harassment
- Schools and local businesses felt the disruption
- The RCMP received a flood of calls reporting the phenomenon
- The Carman RCMP detachment was essentially overwhelmed
- Skepticism and excitement divided the community
- The phenomenon became both a source of local pride and exhaustion

## Media Coverage

**Major Coverage:**
- **Winnipeg Free Press:** Headlines: "Manitoba 'UFO' sighted by hundreds"
- **Carman Valley Leader:** Coined the "Charlie Redstar" name; covered daily from Oct 1974 through Mar 1975
- **Winnipeg Tribune:** Reported extensively on the hotline and mass sighting numbers
- **CBC:** National television coverage; news crews stationed in town for days
- **CTV:** Also sent crews to the area
- **International media:** US wire services picked up the story
- **10,000+ newspaper headlines** in Canada alone

The media coverage was relentless at its peak. It became one of the most publicized UFO events in Canadian history.

## Official Investigation

- **RCMP:** Investigated and documented the phenomenon extensively
- **DND:** Files reviewed by Canadian military; no knowledge of military activity linked to sightings
- **Project Magnet:** Canada's official UFO study program (defunct by 1974 but files maintained) reviewed reports
- **Canadian UFO Survey:** Maintained case files in their database
- **Status:** No definitive explanation was reached. DND files remain partially classified.
- **2008:** CBC revisited the case as one of Manitoba's "greatest unexplained mysteries"

## Skeptical Analysis

### 1. Swamp Gas / Marsh Gas
- Proposed methane or other gases from prairie wetlands ignited and floated upward
- **Problems:** The lights moved against the wind, made intelligent patterns, and traveled too fast for simple gas ignition

### 2. Mass Hysteria / Psychological Feedback Loop
- The "Charlie Redstar" nickname and intense media frenzy may have created self-fulfilling expectations
- The hotline system may have amplified the phenomenon through suggestion
- Once people expected to see something, ordinary phenomena (cars, planes, stars) were reinterpreted as UFOs
- **Strength:** Explains the amplification pattern (more media = more sightings)
- **Weakness:** Does not explain the original October 1974 sightings before media coverage began

### 3. Venus / Astronomical Misidentification
- Some suggested misidentified Venus or bright celestial objects distorted by atmospheric refraction
- **Problems:** The lights moved in ways Venus cannot — intelligent patterns, rapid directional changes, red color (Venus is white/yellow)

### 4. Weather Balloons / Atmospheric Phenomena
- Temperature inversion layers, ice crystals, or atmospheric mirage effects
- Winter inversions are common in Manitoba
- **Weakness:** Does not explain the movement patterns, EM effects, or consistent red color

### 5. Military Aircraft / Secret Testing
- DND investigated; officially stated no knowledge of military activity linked to sightings
- **Status:** Unconfirmed; DND files partially classified

### Consensus Assessment
Most investigators now consider it a combination of:
- An initial genuinely anomalous observation by Len Grant in October 1974
- Mass psychological suggestion amplified by intense media coverage and the hotline feedback loop
- Possible hoaxes (especially in the later months)
- A core of observations that remain genuinely unexplained

## Legacy

- Featured in the **Manitoba Museum** collection
- Referenced by the **Canadian Aviation and Space Museum**
- The **Manitoba UFO Society** maintains the case files and continues research
- The "Charlie Redstar" name has entered Canadian pop culture
- Still discussed as a case study in UFO research, mass hysteria, and media influence on perception
- Often compared to the 1952 Washington D.C. UFO flap and the 1966 Michigan "swamp gas" sightings
